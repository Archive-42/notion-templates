The Most Beautiful Mathematical Formula

Euler’s Identify formula.

Euler’s equation has it all to be the most beautiful mathematical formula to date. Its simple, elegant, it gathers some of the most important mathematical constants, and it has curious mathematical and physical interpretations.

Lets examine it closely.

The Formula

Euler’s Identify formula is the one displayed at the heading of this article. Lets take a look at it here again:

Euler’s Identity. Source.

This three-term formula has amazed mathematicians all over the world since it was coined because the amazing connection it establishes between the different elements involved in it and its different interpretations.

In this post, we will try to remove the magic veil from Euler’s identity, and expose its wonders.

Before that, let's see where this formula comes from.

The History of Euler’s Identity

In the year 1714 British physicist and mathematician Roger Cotes established in one formula the relationship between logarithms, trigonometrical functions and imaginary numbers.

Twenty years later, Leonhard Euler reached the same formula but using exponential functions instead of logarithms. Both formulas, and how to go from one to another are illustrated in the following figure.

Roger’s Cotes equation from 1714 (Top), Euler’s formula from 1748 (Bottom)

It is interesting to note that none of the authors saw the geometrical connotation of their formulas, which we will discuss later.

Now, if we particularize Euler’s formula to the value of x = π, we get the famous Euler’s Identity.

By substituting x for π in Euler’s formula we get Euler’s identity

Let's examine individually the different elements involved in this formula.

The Actors

The first and most important actor is the man whose portrait is shown in Figure just below these lines: Leonhard Euler. His contributions to the world of math and physics range from and graph and number theory to fluid mechanics.

Now, let's get to the formula and its different terms.

  • First, we have the exponential function: e. This function is very special for various reasons: first of all, it is its own derivative.

The derivative of e^x with respect to x

If you see f(x) = e^x as position, and its derivative as position, this means that the speed is always equal to the position. If we are in the position e², then the speed is also e². If we move further away, to e³, then the speed at that point has also changed to e³. Cool right?

Also, if we change the function a little bit, like so, with b being a real number:

The derivative of e^bx with respect to x

Then we get that the velocity is proportional to the speed. Following the same example as before, if b = 2 we would get that at the point where x = 2, we would get e⁴ as position and 2 e⁴ as velocity. The velocity would be twice the speed.

What happens then, if instead of a real number, we plug in an imaginary number, like so:

Derivative of e^ix with respect to x

Following the narrative of the previous examples, let's see what this means for the relationship between position and velocity. For this, we have to go further than our real number perception and expand onto the complex plane. Here is what this looks like:

The complex plane. Source.

Multiplying a real number by i, in the complex plane, means rotating by 90 degrees from the real axis to the imaginary axis. More on this later.

To finish off with e, it is also present in a wide range of physical phenomena, and a key element of radioactivity, electronics, and population growth.

  • Imaginary numbers: i. Initially, numbers were invented to keep track of the count of entire objects. This is how natural numbers were born. Then, a mechanism was needed to keep track of when somebody owed someone else an entire object. Integer numbers were born, which were an extension of the previous natural numbers to the negative side.

After this, the need to keep track of fractions, or parts of entire objects arose, giving birth to the rational numbers. Finally, numbers that describe fractions whose decimals go on forever (like π) were found in mathematics, and thus irrational numbers were born. All of the previous kind of numbers fall under the category of real numbers.

Imaginary numbers are something of another nature.

At their birth, imaginary numbers were conceived as a mathematical tool for being able to operate with squared roots of negative numbers, and the term ‘imaginary’ was kind of derogatory. i, the letter denoting imaginary numbers, is equivalent to the square root of -1.

Value of the imaginary number i

It wasn’t until our beloved Euler showed up, that the square root of -1 was given this letter as a representation, and started being considered useful. After this, it naturally appeared in all kinds of problems from physics like the laws of electricity and magnetism, or wave dynamics.

  • The ratio of the diameter to the circumference of a circle: Pi or π . No introduction is needed for this magic irrational number. It shows up everywhere in maths, and its appearance here, along with the previously introduced actors, and the ones left to describe, is just magnificent.
  • Lastly, we have 0 and 1. The numbers where all the others are derived from. Using these two, you can obtain ANY existing number. Absence and presence. A couple to end this perfect group of ingredients.

Now, let's see how they all fit so beautifully together after a quick demonstration of Euler’s formula.

The tool to do this is the famous Series of Taylor. For those of you who have never heard of them, don’t fear, they are just polynomial approximations of complex functions like the exponential, trigonometrical, logarithmic functions, etc…

The and Geometrical interpretation of Euler’s Formula

To understand the interpretations of this formula, we have to recover the complex plane.

Figure of the complex plane

By looking at this figure, we can see that every angle α gives us a point of a circumference of radius 1: (cos(α), sin(α)). We can also see that cos(α) represents the real part of this point, while sin(α) represents the imaginary part. These kinds of coordinates, defined by a point on a plane with two values from orthogonal directions are called Cartesian coordinates.

How does this relate to Euler’s equation? Let's view it using α.

Euler’s equation defined for α

What this is telling us, is that if we take any number of radians, and plug it into α, what we get is rotation around this circumference of radius 1 of α radians, which in turn would mean we are in the point (cos(α), sin(α)) in Cartesian coordinates.

Euler’s formula allows us to express complex numbers in a more compact way using the exponential. That’s all? Really? Of course not.

The magical thing about the exponential (e) being here, is that if we think of elevating a number to an imaginary exponent as turning α radians around this circumference of radius 1, if we take ANY other number except e and we do this, we will have travelled a certain distance around the circumference like shown in the following figure:

Complex plane with different rotations

Let me explain. When we take the number 2 and elevate it to iα, if α = 1 radian, we would be on the orange point, thus having travelled a distance along the circumference from the grey ‘start’ point to the orange point, along the yellow line of 0,693.

If we do this for the number 5, we would have travelled a distance from the grey point to the green point of 1,609. However, if we use the magical number e the distance we would have travelled is 1, exactly the same as the value we have given α. Woah.

But hold on, it does not end here. If we start at the point (1,0) of the real axis, and we give α the value π, we would have turned π radians (half of the circumference), we would have completed a distance along this circumference of π, and Euler’s equation would have its identity form:

Conclusion

Euler’s Identity is one of the most beautiful formulas of our days. Gauss, the famous mathematician is reported to have commented that if this formula were not immediately obvious to someone reading it, he would never be a first-class mathematician.

On the opposite side, in 1800, another mathematician, Benjamin Price, said about Euler’s Identity:

It is absolutely paradoxical; we cannot understand it, and we don’t know what it means, but we have proved it, and therefore we know it must be the truth.

It is a wonderful formula for how it combines together all the different elements, and for its meaning. However, not everything that there is to Euler’s formula has been explained in this article.

For example, how does it fit with the concept of exponentiation that we have of repeatedly multiplying a number? Isn’t 2⁵ meant to be 2 multiplied 5 times by itself? What is all this nonsense of rotating?
