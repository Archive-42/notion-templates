Introduction

Every generation goes through periods of time that define that generation’s attitude to the world, their view of themselves, and their view of their place in the world. In their formative years, Boomers had Vietnam and rock, Gen X had latchkeys and the Gulf War, and the Millennials had the Great Financial Crisis.

Last week, the United States Capitol was stormed for the first time since 1812. The Confederate flag made it deeper into the Capitol than during the Civil War. This will be one of the most significant events for my generation.

I like to think about the future — first by understanding where we are today and the direction we’re going, then defining where we should go, and finally figuring out how we need to act in order to get there. Looking at the various implications of this terrorist attack on the future, I have very few answers. Instead, these really are my initial thoughts and the questions I’m asking myself as I try to make sense of the world.

What Didn’t Happen

The events of the Storming of the Capitol are still being pieced together from the timing of the events to the chants to the lack of response. However, the most important parts to me are the events that didn’t happen, but very easily could have.

As horrifying as this terrorist attack was, it could have been much worse. From a completely literal perspective, a bunch of people in costumes walked in, yelled some stuff, and walked out. And Congress took a 10-hour break and came back to do what they were going to do anyway. Of course, the storming has many symbolic impacts, not to mention the psychological impacts on politicians and their hard-working, underpaid staff.

However, the lack of real, tangible outcomes seems to be driving the lack of response from Republicans. Several of my Trump-supporting friends argued that in terms of physical damage, Black Lives Matter protests in the summer of 2020 left more destruction.. This isn’t only anecdotal — 147 Republicans still voted to carry on the ridiculous charade of contesting the election, but also an Economist/YouGov poll on January 6th found that 68% of Republicans did not think the storming of the Capitol represented a threat to democracy. And while 43% of Republicans opposed the terrorist attack, 45% said they strongly or somewhat supported it. It can’t be taken for granted that everyone is taking this seriously.

Humans tend to focus on tangible events that happened, rather than the broader possibility space of events that were just as likely to happen, but for whatever reason did not occur. Everyone remembers the terrorist attacks on 9/11, nobody remembers when Vasili Alexandrovich Arkhipov singlehandedly averted global nuclear war.

As we later discovered, the crowd at the Capitol had improvised explosive devices, long guns, Molotov cocktails, and zip ties. And as we later discovered, they were about a minute away from breaching the Senate chambers. Meanwhile, the people ranked 1-2-3 in the line of succession were in the building. They say when someone tells you who they are, believe them. When a mob tells you that they want to kill the Vice President, believe them.

None of these more violent events happened, but the fact that they didn’t happen isn’t a reason to take this less seriously. This is really a complex systems point — it might have been a variety of really trivial factors that derailed these plans. Maybe one of the ringleaders didn’t make the riots because he got in a car accident the night before, maybe the mob decided to take one door instead of another. We have no idea how close we came to having political leaders kidnapped and executed.

Networks vs. Hierarchies

Several days after the storming of the Capitol, when some of the initial horror subsided, I began to sit back and reflected with awe at last week’s events. A loosely organized network of extremist groups was radicalized and activated via social media, and nearly subverted the United States government, the most powerful hierarchy in the world.

This is part of a broader long wave trend, going far beyond today’s Republican party. In Revolt of the Public, Martin Gurri outlines the role of social media in enabling the Twitter Revolutions throughout the Middle East in which citizens banded together to end the reign of several long-standing dictators and in which the Muslim Brotherhood rose. The 2010’s also involved various other movements including Occupy Wall Street and the rise of the Tea Party in the United States which set the stage for Donald Trump’s rise. And ultimately, the Trump campaign successfully but nefariously used social media, leveraging stolen data, leveraging microtargeting, and ‘lookalike audiences’. However, it was President Barack Obama’s 2008 campaign that successfully used social media in a political campaign. The same technology that made people believe in change and toppled ruthless dictators enabled this terrorist attack on American democracy.

But, it’s not just a matter of the rise of communications technology. Peter Thiel points to a breakdown in the assumptions that undergird our current system. The fact that society is no longer innovating as quickly means we cannot assume we have relentless growth. Regarding the Arab Spring, Thiel says, “We may embellish the 2011 Arab Spring as the hopeful by-product of the information age, but we should not downplay the primary role of runaway food prices and of the many desperate people who became more hungry than scared.”

In Western society, the same underlying issues around growth and innovation represent an existential threat: “The give-and-take of Western democracies depends on the idea that we can craft political solutions that enable most people to win most of the time. But in a world without growth, we can expect a loser for every winner.”

The irony of Donald Trump is that he spoke to real issues, like slowing economic growth in many parts of the country, but then did little to solve them. There are underlying issues in all of these cases and information technology amplifies the discontent enabling flash insurrections. This certainly won’t be the last time these powerful technologies will amplify terrorists in the US or in other countries, and it’s naive to think that governments will learn from this.

Strategically, this is a David and Goliath story. Governments are nominally much more powerful than these But as Lawrence Freedman says in Strategy, A History, the core of David’s strategy is surprise: “He took a stone out of his bag ‘and slung it and smote the Philistine in his forehead, so that the stone sunk into his forehead. And he fell upon his face to the earth.’ David then took the giant’s sword to kill him and cut off his head. When the Philistines saw their champion was dead, they fled. David’s success depended on surprise and accuracy.”

David’s strategy could only be used once; if Goliath hadn’t died or if the Philistines hadn’t fled, the story would’ve been very different. And yet, modern hierarchies either aren’t able to learn or if they do learn, they aren’t able to respond. Modern hierarchies are perpetually Goliaths.

Rationality, Truth, and Empathy

For many years now, there has been a long line of people proclaiming we are now living in a post-truth world. But it’s never totally clear when we lived in a world of truth. There has always been disagreement on the facts, often with little ability to verify the truth. To the extent that there was some shared narrative, the narrative was dominated by those in power. Most of all, there has always been polarization. As Ezra Klein writes, today’s polarization is sorted by party and seems much more apparent. But while the 20th century is often seen as a heyday of political cooperation, those relations were a Faustian bargain, involving the integration of the Dixiecrats into the Democratic party.

Even throughout the 20th century, the idea of post-truth was being talked about in various forms, whether it was Friedrich Nietzsche or Hitler talking about the Big Lie. While I don’t think something has fundamentally changed in the fabric of our reality, something has changed in our experience.

The experience of seeing other rational people holding such drastically different views of the world has ever been so visceral. Most people spend most of their time in their own filter bubbles, but every now and then those bubbles are punctured, whether it’s coming across a Facebook group with hundreds of thousands of people looking at reality completely differently or seeing thousands of people storm the United States Capitol. Often it’s our families, our friends, and trusted people in our communities who happen to also espouse these radical views. Contrary to certain narratives, these people aren’t stupid or even necessarily more irrational than anyone else. For example, the Q-Anon conspiracy theory is extraordinarily complex — in fact, its wide-ranging complexity as a melting pot of all conspiracies is what has made it so popular.

For that reason, keeping tabs on Q-Anon Twitter is fascinating; sometimes people who follow the conspiracy will cross-reference a random, overlooked symbol in some Q post from many years ago with a recent Trump tweet to tell a new story that affirms their beliefs and their identities. And the experience of seeing friends and otherwise normal people fall into these rabbit holes is jarring. The New York Times podcast by the same name, The Rabbit Hole, follows various people who went from watching one Q-Anon YouTube video to becoming enamored by the strong community to becoming fully engrossed in the stories and theories.

Sometimes, I’m even wary of reading too deeply into these things, just in case my equally fallible brain with all of the same cognitive biases might fall into the rabbit hole. This is life in the 21st century. Another long wave of history has been the cyclical prioritization and deprioritization of rationality. We all have our delusions, we all trick ourselves in one way or another. The visceral experience of seeing rationality lead others to such radical views makes it difficult to trust my own rationality as a basis for understanding the world.

For these reasons and more, it’s always worth having empathy for others rather than defaulting to insulting their intelligence. If everyone around you holds a certain worldview, wouldn’t you be much more open to considering it? Even more, if you really, truly believed in a conspiracy such as Pizzagate, wouldn’t it only be rational and honorable to storm the pizza shop?

Pay Attention

A recent, perverse development in Silicon Valley culture is this idea that not paying attention to politics is a productivity hack. It’s not. It’s simply ignorant. Now, it's a personal choice whether “paying attention” means staring at Twitter all day or engaging with history or something else altogether. Regardless, being educated in whichever way you see fit is virtuous.

I often find myself thinking back to this video by Lex Fridman where he talks about struggling through a tome about the Third Reich. Some people would tell you that reading such literature is infotainment that should be avoided since it isn’t immediately useful to you. But Lex says that he’s preparing. He’s preparing in case there is a time in his life where he is called upon to stand up to evil, as many did during the Third Reich. In some ways, when it’s most difficult to pay attention, that’s exactly when you should. But when things are going well, that’s the best time to proactively educate yourself. It might even be a universal imperative.

I often come back to this quote:

“Hard times create strong men. Strong men create good times. Good times create weak men. And, weak men create hard times.”

It seems to me this doesn’t have to be true, as long as we use the good times well.
