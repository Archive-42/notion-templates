Giving Talks

I give quite a lot of talks‚Äîto a very wide range of audiences. I particularly like giving talks about subjects I haven‚Äôt talked about before. I remember the very first computer projector I used, in 1980. It was a Hughes ‚Äúliquid crystal light valve‚Äù, and once I got it connected to a CRT terminal, it worked beautifully. And as a result of that very experience I decided I‚Äôd better actually understand how computers talk to projectors. Computer operating systems have gotten much better about handling this in recent years, but for high-profile, high-production-value events, I have a little box that spoofs EDID strings to force my computer to send a specific signal, regardless of what the projector seems to be asking it for.

Some of the talks I give are completely spontaneous. But often I‚Äôll have notes‚Äîand occasionally even a script. And I‚Äôll always write these in a Wolfram Notebook. Some of my thinking about computer filesystems derives from my experience in the 1970s and 1980s with physical filesystems. Back when I was a teenager doing physics I voraciously made photocopies of papers. And at first I thought the best way to file these papers would be in lots of different categories, with each category stored in a different physical file folder. I thought hard about the categories, often feeling quite pleased with the cleverness of associating a particular paper with a particular category. And I had the principle that if too many papers accumulated in one category, I should break it up into new categories.

All this at first seemed like a good idea. But fairly quickly I realized it wasn‚Äôt. Because too often when I wanted to find a particular paper I couldn‚Äôt figure out just what cleverness had caused me to associate it with what category. And the result was that I completely changed my approach. Instead of insisting on narrow categories, I allowed broad, general categories‚Äîwith the result that I could easily have 50 or more papers filed in a single category (often ending up with multiple well-stuffed physical file folders for a given category):

And, yes, that meant that I would sometimes have to leaf through 50 papers or more to find one I wanted. But realistically this wouldn‚Äôt take more than a few minutes. I have pretty much the same principle about some parts of my computer filesystem today. These days my filesystem is broken into an active part (that I continuously sync onto all my computers), and a more archival part, that I keep on a central fileserver (and that, for example, contains my older-generation filesystems).

There are only a few top-level folders in my active filesystem. One is called Events. Its subfolders are years. Another top-level folder is called Designs. It contains all my notes about my design work on the Wolfram Language and other things we‚Äôre building. And in fact this is a general principle in the project-oriented parts of my filesystem. Every folder has a subfolder called ARCHIVES. As it happens, this approach is somewhat inspired by something I saw done with physical documents. When I was consulting at Bell Labs in the early 1980s I saw that a friend of mine had two garbage cans in his office. When I asked him why, he explained that one was for genuine garbage and the other was a buffer into which he would throw documents that he thought he‚Äôd probably never want again. He‚Äôd let the buffer garbage can fill up, and once it was full, he‚Äôd throw away the lower documents in it, since from the fact that he hadn‚Äôt fished them out, he figured he‚Äôd probably never miss them if they were thrown away permanently.

Needless to say, I don‚Äôt follow exactly this approach, and in fact I keep everything, digital or paper. But the point is that the ARCHIVES mechanism gives me a way to easily keep material while still making it easy to see everything that‚Äôs active.

I have a bunch of other conventions too. When I‚Äôm doing designs, I‚Äôll typically keep my notes in files with names like Notes-01.nb or SWNotes-01.nb.
